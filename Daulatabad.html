DAULATABAD FORT
|Information :

Daulatabad Fort, originally known as Devagiri Fort, is a magnificent 14th-century fort situated in Maharashtra. It stands on a conical hill about 200 meters high, making it one of the most powerful forts of its time. The fort's strategic location and impressive defenses made it virtually impregnable for centuries.


The fort complex includes several notable structures spread over approximately 94 hectares. It was the capital of the Yadava dynasty and later the Tughlaq dynasty. The fort is renowned for its ingenious defense mechanisms, including a complex series of entrances designed to confuse attackers, and a steep, smooth slope to prevent elephant charges.


Daulatabad Fort is particularly famous for its brilliant defense architecture. The fort has a 5 km long wall, and the moat around the fort was once filled with crocodiles. The Chand Minar, a 30-meter high tower, stands as a testament to the fort's glorious past and serves as a watchtower.


The fort's most remarkable feature is its defense system which includes a series of secret escape routes, false doorways, and a complicated maze-like pathway to the top. The fort never fell to siege, and was only captured through treachery or abandonment.

|History and local lore :

The fort was built in 1187 by the Yadava king Bhillama V. In 1296, it was captured by Ala-ud-din Khilji, and later became the capital of the Tughlaq dynasty under Muhammad bin Tughluq in 1327. The sultan renamed it Daulatabad ("City of Prosperity") and forcibly moved the entire population of Delhi here, though the capital was shifted back to Delhi two years later.


The fort changed hands several times between the Bahmani Sultanate, the Nizam Shahis of Ahmadnagar, the Mughals, and the Marathas. Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj captured it in 1653 but it was later taken by the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb. The Nizam of Hyderabad held it until it came under Indian control after independence.

|Things to look out :

Daulatabad Fort is an architectural marvel with several unique features that visitors should not miss:


  • The Defense System: The fort's brilliant defense mechanisms include a complicated series of entrances, false gates, and a steep, smooth slope designed to prevent elephant charges.

  • Chand Minar: This 30-meter high tower built in 1445 by Ala-ud-din Bahmani served as a victory tower and a watchtower.

  • The Dark Passage: A pitch-dark, zigzagging tunnel designed to confuse invaders, now lit with electric lights for visitors.

  • Baradari: The remains of the royal palace with twelve doors, offering panoramic views of the surrounding area.

  • Hathi Haud: A massive water tank that could hold 10,000 cubic meters of water, used to supply the fort during sieges.

  • Cannon Fortifications: Several ancient cannons still positioned along the fort walls, including the famous 'Daulatabad Cannon'.
|Key facts :
  • Built in 1187 by the Yadava king Bhillama V

  • Originally named Devagiri ("Hill of Gods")

  • Renamed Daulatabad ("City of Prosperity") by Muhammad bin Tughluq in 1327

  • The fort walls extend approximately 5 km in circumference

  • The fort stands on a 200-meter high conical hill

  • Never conquered by force, only through treachery or abandonment

|Nearby places :

While visiting Daulatabad Fort, consider exploring these nearby attractions:


  • Ellora Caves: Just 15 km away, these UNESCO World Heritage rock-cut temples date from 600-1000 CE and represent Buddhist, Hindu and Jain traditions.

  • Bibi Ka Maqbara: Located in Aurangabad (13 km away), this 17th century tomb is often called the "Poor Man's Taj Mahal".

  • Aurangabad Caves: About 15 km from the fort, these 6th-7th century Buddhist caves feature beautiful sculptures.

  • Grishneshwar Temple: One of the 12 Jyotirlingas, this important Shiva temple is located 30 km from the fort.
